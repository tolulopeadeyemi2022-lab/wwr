Three years ago, we sat at our kitchen table with a calculator, two laptops, and a growing sense of dread. Between credit cards, a car loan, and leftover student loans, we owed just over $38,000. Neither of us had a plan. We just knew the minimum payments were eating our paychecks and going nowhere.

That night became the start of our debt free journey. It wasn't glamorous, and it definitely wasn't fast at first. But three years later, we're debt free, and looking back, a handful of decisions made the biggest difference. We're not financial experts, just two people who got tired of feeling like our paychecks belonged to everyone but us. These are the debt free journey tips that actually worked for us, not the polished version you see in a highlight reel, but what genuinely moved the needle.

## Where We Started

Here's what our debt looked like in year one:

* **Credit cards:** $9,200 at an average of 21% interest
* **Car loan:** $14,800 at 6.5% interest
* **Student loans:** $14,000 at 5% interest

Our combined take-home pay was around $5,600 a month. On paper, that sounds like enough room to make progress. In reality, minimum payments alone came to $780 a month, and we were barely scraping by without touching the principal in any meaningful way.

## Tip 1: The \"One Category\" Rule for Budgeting

When we started, we tried to cut everything. No streaming services, no coffees, no cheap takeout, no new clothes. We lasted exactly twelve days before dropping the entire plan and buying an expensive dinner because we were exhausted and frustrated.

Total deprivation doesn't work. What worked instead was picking just *one* variable spending category to slash drastically while keeping the rest reasonable. 

We chose dining out. We didn't cut it to zero, but we limited it to once a week and moved the rest of that budget category—about $300 a month—straight toward our target debt. Leaving our small streaming subscriptions and grocery runs alone kept us sane enough to stick to the plan for thirty-six months.

## Tip 2: We Used the Avalanche Method, and Stopped Second-Guessing It

We spent weeks debating whether to use the debt snowball method (paying the smallest balance first) or the debt avalanche method (paying the highest interest rate first). 

Ultimately, we picked the avalanche method because that 21% interest on our credit cards was eating us alive. We focused every extra dollar on the credit card balance first while paying the bare minimums on the car and student loans. Once that card hit zero, we rolled the entire payment amount into the car loan, and finally into the student loans.

The key wasn't finding the perfect method. The key was picking one strategy and refusing to change our minds halfway through when progress felt slow.

## Tip 3: Stop the Automatic Upgrades

During our second year, my partner got a promotion that came with a $400 monthly raise after taxes. Old habits would have seen us moving into a slightly nicer apartment or upgrading our aging car. 

Instead, we committed to "lifestyle freeze." We kept our living expenses exactly where they were and set up an automatic transfer to send that extra $400 straight to our active debt payoff target every month. If you don't see the money hit your primary checking account, you don't build a habit of spending it.

## Tip 4: Celebrate the Invisible Milestones

Waiting until you reach absolute zero to celebrate makes a long debt-free journey feel grueling. We set small checkposts every $5,000 we cleared. 

When we hit a milestone, we didn't go out and spend $200 on a lavish dinner—that would defeat the point—but we would rent a movie we wanted to see, pick up a specific dessert, or take a day trip to a state park. Acknowledging the progress kept us from treating the process like a life sentence.

## Common Blunders We Made Along the Way

* **Not having an emergency fund buffer at the start.** We threw every cent at our debt in month three, only for our car alternator to fail. Because we had zero savings, that $450 bill went right back onto the credit card we were trying to pay off. It felt completely defeating. Build a $1,000 starter buffer before attacking debt aggressively.
* **Hiding the journey from our close friends.** We turned down invitations with vague excuses because we were embarrassed to say we were budgeting. Once we honestly told our circle, "We're trying to wipe out our debt this year, so we're keeping spending low," they shifted to hosting casual potlucks and game nights instead. Our relationships improved and so did our follow-through.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How long does a typical debt free journey take?** It depends entirely on your income, debt amount, and interest rates. Ours took three years for $38,000, but timelines vary widely, and a slower timeline isn't a failure.

**Should we have built savings before paying off debt?** In hindsight, yes, at least a small buffer. A $500 to $1,000 emergency fund before aggressively paying down debt can prevent new debt from unexpected expenses.

**Did you use the snowball or avalanche method?** We used the avalanche method, targeting the highest interest rate first. It saved us money over time, though the snowball method's faster early wins work better for some people's motivation.

**What kept you motivated through slower months?** Automating payments removed the daily decision fatigue, and celebrating smaller payoffs along the way kept the process from feeling like one long wait for a distant finish line.

## Key Takeaways

Our debt free journey wasn't fast in the beginning, and it wasn't perfect. What actually worked was writing down real numbers, targeting the highest interest debt first, cutting one category hard instead of everything a little, and building in small celebrations along the way.

If you're at the start of your own journey, begin exactly where we did. Pull every statement, write down every balance, and pick just one thing to change this week. That single step is where real progress actually starts.
