You don't need $10,000 sitting in a savings account to start investing. You don't even need $100. Most brokerages today let you open an account with zero dollars and start buying shares with as little as $5.

Figuring out how to invest with little money isn't about finding a shortcut. It's about understanding that the amount you start with matters far less than the fact that you started at all.

## Why Starting Small Still Works

A single dollar invested at 25 has decades to grow before retirement. Time, not the size of your first contribution, does most of the heavy lifting in building wealth. Waiting for a bigger paycheck before you start investing usually backfires. Every year you delay isn't just a year of missed contributions; it's a year of missed growth.

## Step 1: Open an Account That Doesn't Require Much

Look for a brokerage with no account minimum and no maintenance fees. Most major platforms let you open a brokerage account or a Roth IRA without depositing a large sum up front. 

* **Robo-advisors:** These are excellent if you want to be hands-off. Many allow you to start with as little as $1 and build a diversified portfolio automatically based on your age and goals.
* **Account Type:** If you are investing for retirement, a Roth IRA is often the best choice because your withdrawals in retirement are tax-free.

## Step 2: Use Fractional Shares

A decade ago, buying one share of a high-priced stock required saving hundreds or thousands of dollars. Today, most brokerages let you buy a fraction of a share for whatever dollar amount you have. If you have $25 to invest, you can own a piece of a fund that costs much more per share. This removes the barrier of entry for small investors.

## Step 3: Automate Small, Consistent Contributions

Consistency matters more than the size of any single contribution. Setting up an automatic transfer of $25 or $50 a week removes the decision-making process. 

Automating small amounts also protects you from trying to time the market. This practice, known as dollar-cost averaging, ensures you buy more shares when prices are low and fewer when prices are high, which helps remove emotion from your investment strategy.

## Step 4: Choose Low-Cost, Diversified Investments

With a small amount of money, you want your investments doing as much work as possible. Avoid individual stocks, which concentrate your risk. Instead, choose a total market index fund or an S&P 500 index fund to spread your money across hundreds or thousands of companies.

Always pay attention to the expense ratio, which is the annual fee charged as a percentage of your investment. A small difference in fees can compound into significant costs over decades.

## Key Takeaways

You don't need a large sum of money to start investing. Open an account with no minimum, use fractional shares, automate small contributions, and choose low-cost diversified funds. Increase your contribution as your income grows, and let time do the rest.

## Your Next Step

Open a brokerage account or Roth IRA this week, even if you only have $25 to put into it. Set up an automatic transfer for whatever amount feels manageable. The size of your first contribution matters far less than how many years it has to grow.
