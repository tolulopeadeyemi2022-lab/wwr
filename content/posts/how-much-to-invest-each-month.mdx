There's no magic number that works for everyone. Someone earning $45,000 a year and someone earning $145,000 a year shouldn't be investing the same dollar amount, and honestly, they probably shouldn't use the same percentage either.

Figuring out how much to invest each month isn't about copying a rule you read somewhere. It's about looking at your actual income, your actual expenses, and your actual goals, then landing on a number you can stick with for years. That last part matters more than people realize. The best investing plan is the one you don't abandon after three rough months.

## Start With a Percentage, Not a Dollar Amount

Most financial guidance points toward investing somewhere between 10% and 20% of your gross income. That range exists for a reason. It's high enough to build real wealth over a career, and low enough that most people can actually sustain it without wrecking their day-to-day life.

If you earn $60,000 a year, 15% works out to $750 a month. If you earn $90,000, that same 15% becomes $1,125 a month. The percentage stays consistent even though the dollar figure changes, which is exactly why thinking in percentages beats picking a random dollar amount.

## The Hierarchy of Where That Money Goes

Once you decide on a percentage, you need to know where to put it. There is a generally accepted hierarchy that maximizes your growth while protecting your downside:

1.  **Capture the Employer Match:** If your job offers a 401(k) match, put enough in to get the full amount. This is an instant 100% return on your money. No other investment in the world offers this.
2.  **Emergency Fund:** Before you ramp up to 15% or 20%, ensure you have a starter emergency fund. Without it, the first unexpected car repair or medical bill will force you to withdraw your investments or reach for a high-interest credit card.
3.  **High-Interest Debt:** If you have credit card debt at 20%+ interest, pay that off before increasing your monthly investment contributions beyond the employer match. It is mathematically impossible to out-invest a 20% interest rate.
4.  **Tax-Advantaged Accounts:** Once your match is captured and high-interest debt is cleared, prioritize Roth IRAs or Traditional IRAs to take advantage of tax-free or tax-deferred growth.

## Adjusting for Your Specific Life Stage

Your goals change as your life does, and your investing percentage should adjust accordingly.

* **Early Career (20s):** You have the most valuable asset: time. Even a smaller percentage invested now, thanks to compounding, can grow significantly. Focus on building the habit of contributing consistently.
* **Mid-Career (30s-40s):** Life gets more expensive with housing, families, and career growth. If you can’t hit 15% right now, don't panic. Invest what you can, and make a plan to increase your contribution by 1% each year.
* **Late Career (50s+):** Catch-up contributions become essential. You may need a higher percentage of your income to make up for lost time if your accounts aren't where you want them to be.



## Key Takeaways

There's no universal answer to how much to invest each month. Start with 10% to 20% of your income as a baseline, adjust based on your debt and emergency savings, and always capture your full employer match first. The right number is the one that fits your life and that you'll actually keep contributing.

## Your Next Step

Open your last pay stub and calculate what 15% of your gross income looks like in dollars. Compare that to what you're investing right now. If there's a gap, increase your contribution by even one percent this month. You don't need the perfect number today. You need a number you'll still be using next year.
