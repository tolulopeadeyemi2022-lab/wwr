Deciding whether paying off debt early is worth it depends on a careful balance between your interest rates, long-term investment goals, and personal peace of mind.

## When Paying Off Debt Early Makes Sense
High-interest debt—such as credit cards carrying 22% rates—should be targeted immediately because every extra dollar sent toward the balance yields a guaranteed, high-return savings equivalent. 

## When Investing Comes Ahead
Low-interest debt, like a mortgage or federal student loans below 4%, is often considered cheap money. In these scenarios, capturing full employer retirement matches and investing surplus cash usually builds more wealth over the long run.

## A Practical Decision Framework
1. **Target high-interest debt** above 7% first.
2. **Capture employer retirement matches** to avoid leaving free money behind.
3. **Build an emergency cushion** of $500 to $1,000 before aggressive payoff.
4. **Evaluate personal comfort** with remaining low-interest debt to balance emotional stress against mathematical returns.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
* **Forgoing employer retirement matches** to prepay low-interest debt.
* **Draining your entire emergency fund** and risking new debt from unexpected expenses.
* **Treating all debts equally** regardless of interest rate disparities.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is it always worth paying off debt early?
No. High-interest debt benefits greatly from early payoff, whereas low-interest loans often make more sense to pay on schedule while investing the difference.

### Does paying off debt early hurt my credit score?
It can cause a minor, temporary dip—especially if an account is closed—which typically recovers within a few months.

## Key Takeaways
Weighing early debt payoff against investing relies on analyzing your interest rates, securing employer retirement matches, and factoring in your personal tolerance for financial stress.

## What To Do Next
Review your loan statements today to list every interest rate, identifying any balance above 7% as your immediate primary target.
