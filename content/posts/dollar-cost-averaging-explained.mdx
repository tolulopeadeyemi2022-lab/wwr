The fear of buying just before a market drop keeps many people out of the stock market entirely. Dollar cost averaging solves this problem by allowing you to invest on a fixed schedule, removing the need to predict market highs or lows.

## Why Trying to Time the Market Backfires
No one can consistently predict market movements, and waiting for the "perfect moment" usually means keeping your money in a low-interest checking account where inflation erodes its value. Dollar cost averaging replaces guesswork with a repeatable, disciplined plan.

## What Is Dollar Cost Averaging?
Dollar cost averaging is an investing strategy where you invest a fixed dollar amount at regular intervals, regardless of whether prices are up or down. This ensures that you automatically buy more shares when prices are low and fewer when they are high, which averages out your cost per share over time.

## How to Start Dollar Cost Averaging
* Determine a consistent amount: Choose a monthly amount you can commit to without financial stress.
* Pick your investment: Use index funds or ETFs to ensure your money is spread across hundreds of companies.
* Select a schedule: Align your investing schedule with your pay cycle to make it easier to maintain.
* Automate it: Set up automatic transfers from your bank to your brokerage account to ensure you never skip a contribution.
* Leave it alone: Resist the urge to check prices daily and stick to the plan through market dips and rallies.

## A Real Example
If you invest $300 every month for a year:
* When the share price is $50, your $300 buys 6 shares.
* If the price drops to $40, your $300 buys 7.5 shares.
* If the price climbs to $60, your $300 buys 5 shares.
By staying consistent, you naturally purchase more shares when they are cheaper.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
* Stopping during market drops: This is when the strategy is most effective because you are buying more shares at a discount.
* Treating it as a profit guarantee: This strategy reduces risk but does not guarantee gains or protect against long-term market downturns.
* Checking too often: Monitoring daily swings creates unnecessary anxiety.
* Investing short-term funds: Only invest money you do not need for at least five years.

## Does It Beat Lump Sum Investing?
While research suggests that investing a lump sum immediately can sometimes outperform dollar cost averaging, most beginners do not have large sums available. Dollar cost averaging is primarily a behavior strategy that keeps you investing steadily instead of freezing up due to market uncertainty.

## Your Next Step
Open your brokerage account and set up one automatic monthly transfer. This single action establishes a habit that builds your future wealth in the background while you focus on your daily life.
