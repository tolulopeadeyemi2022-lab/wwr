A $2,000 credit card balance at 24% interest costs you roughly $480 a year just to exist. Prioritizing high interest debt stops this silent drain on your future.

## Why High Interest Debt Deserves Your Attention First
Interest compounds over time, meaning high-rate balances keep more of your monthly payment tied up in interest charges rather than shrinking the principal. 

## The Debt Avalanche Method
The most mathematically efficient strategy is the debt avalanche method:
1. **List every debt** with its interest rate and minimum payment.
2. **Order debts from highest interest rate to lowest**.
3. **Pay minimums on everything** except the top-priority high-rate balance.
4. **Throw every extra dollar** at that single high-rate debt until it is fully cleared.
5. **Roll the payment over** to the next highest rate and repeat.

## What "High Interest" Means
Generally, any rate above 8%—including most credit cards, store cards, and payday loans—deserves immediate avalanche prioritization ahead of investing or lower-rate loans.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
* **Paying only the minimum**, which keeps balances permanently stagnant.
* **Opening new credit lines** that risk doubling your total burden.
* **Investing while carrying massive interest rates** that outpace typical market returns.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Should I save for emergencies before paying off high interest debt?
Keep a small $500 to $1,000 starter buffer so unexpected costs don't force you right back onto your credit cards.

### Is avalanche always better than snowball?
Mathematically, the avalanche method saves more money, though some prefer the snowball method for quick behavioral wins.

## Key Takeaways
Targeting high interest debt first through the avalanche method stops compounding interest charges, saves money over time, and accelerates your path to financial freedom.
