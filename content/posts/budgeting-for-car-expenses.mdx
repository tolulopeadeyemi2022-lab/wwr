Most people budget for their car based only on their loan payment and insurance premium. However, car repairs are inevitable and often arrive as lump-sum costs that can destabilize a budget. Learning how to budget for car expenses—beyond the predictable monthly bills—is what separates a mildly annoying repair from one that wrecks your entire month.

## The Cost-Per-Mile Approach
If you are unsure how much to save, estimate your costs forward using a cost-per-mile approach. Industry estimates suggest total maintenance and repair costs—excluding fuel and insurance—fall between 8 and 12 cents per mile for the average vehicle[cite: 9].

* **Example**: If you drive 12,000 miles a year, that translates to roughly $960 to $1,440 annually, or $80 to $120 per month[cite: 9]. 

## Three Ways to Build Your Fund
1. **Flat Monthly Approach**: Automate a round number, such as $100 per month, regardless of your driving habits[cite: 9]. This is the simplest method to maintain.
2. **Percentage-of-Value Approach**: Save 1 percent of your car's value annually toward maintenance[cite: 9]. Note that this may underestimate costs for older vehicles.
3. **Mileage-Based Approach**: Multiply your annual mileage by 8 to 12 cents and divide by 12 to determine your monthly contribution[cite: 9]. This method adjusts naturally as your driving habits change.

## Setting Up the System
* **Dedicated Account**: Open a savings account exclusively for car-related costs, separate from your general emergency fund[cite: 9].
* **Automation**: Automate your monthly transfer so the fund grows without requiring manual decisions[cite: 9].
* **High-Yield Savings**: Since car repairs are not usually same-day emergencies, a high-yield savings account allows you to capture extra interest while the money sits unused[cite: 9].
* **Buffer for Known Costs**: Mark registration, inspection, and tire replacement dates on your calendar. Increase your contributions slightly in the months leading up to these events[cite: 9].

## What to Do When the Fund Runs Short
If a repair exceeds your current balance, cover the gap from your general emergency fund rather than using a credit card[cite: 9]. Afterward, prioritize rebuilding both funds. A single large repair does not mean the system failed; it means your fund successfully absorbed a financial shock[cite: 9].

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
* **Waiting for data**: You do not need a full year of repair history to start; begin with a mileage-based estimate and adjust as you go[cite: 9].
* **Ignoring the fund because of a warranty**: Warranties rarely cover routine maintenance, tires, or brakes[cite: 9]. A smaller fund is still necessary.
* **Pausing contributions**: Keep contributing consistently even during "quiet" years to prepare for the inevitable repairs of the following year[cite: 9].

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How much should a car maintenance fund hold?**
Many financial planners suggest keeping a cushion of $500 to $1,000 available at all times, in addition to your ongoing monthly contributions[cite: 9].

**Should I stop contributing once I reach a certain amount?**
It is reasonable to pause or reduce contributions once you have built a comfortable cushion, such as $1,500 to $2,000, then resume smaller contributions to maintain that level[cite: 9].

## Key Takeaways
Budgeting for car expenses means treating repairs as predictable costs with an uncertain timeline rather than random emergencies. By automating a dedicated fund, you remove the financial shock the next time something breaks.

## Your Next Step
