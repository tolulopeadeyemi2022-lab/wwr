A 401(k) is a retirement account offered through your employer that allows you to contribute a percentage of your paycheck to be invested for your future. It acts as a container for investments, such as mutual funds or target-date funds, which grow over your career.

## Traditional vs. Roth 401(k)
*   **Traditional 401(k):** Contributions are taken out pre-tax, lowering your taxable income today, but withdrawals in retirement are taxed as income.
*   **Roth 401(k):** Contributions are made with money that has already been taxed, meaning you do not get an immediate tax break, but your withdrawals in retirement are completely tax-free.

## Employer Matching: The Closest Thing to Free Money
If your employer offers a match, it is an automatic return on your investment that no other account can promise. You should always contribute at least enough to capture the full match before investing in other accounts.

## Vesting: The Fine Print
Your personal contributions are always yours. However, employer-matched funds may follow a vesting schedule, requiring you to work at the company for a certain number of years before that money fully belongs to you.

## 2026 Contribution Limits
*   **Standard Limit:** Employees can contribute up to $24,500 annually.
*   **Catch-up Contributions:** Those 50 and older can contribute an additional $8,000. 
*   **Age 60-63 Catch-up:** A new, higher catch-up limit of $11,250 is available for this age group under SECURE 2.0 legislation.
*   **High Earners:** Employees earning over $150,000 in the prior year must make catch-up contributions on a Roth basis if the plan offers it.

## Choosing Your Investments
Most beginners should opt for a target-date fund, which automatically adjusts your investment mix to be more aggressive early on and more conservative as you approach retirement. If your plan does not offer this, look for low-cost S&P 500 or total market index funds, paying close attention to the expense ratio.

## Changing Jobs
If you leave your company, you do not lose your 401(k). You can leave it where it is, roll it into a new employer's 401(k), or roll it into an IRA. Avoid cashing out the balance, as this triggers income taxes and potential early withdrawal penalties.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
*   **Missing the match:** Always contribute enough to secure the full employer match.
*   **Leaving money in cash:** Ensure your contributions are invested in a fund rather than sitting as uninvested cash in your account.
*   **Cashing out early:** This erases years of growth and subjects you to unnecessary taxes and penalties.
*   **Ignoring fees:** High expense ratios quietly erode your returns over the long run.

## Your Next Step
Log into your 401(k) account to confirm that your contribution rate is sufficient to capture the full employer match and that your balance is actively invested in a fund matching your timeline.
