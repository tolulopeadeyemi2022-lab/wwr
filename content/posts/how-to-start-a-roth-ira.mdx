You don't need a finance degree to open a Roth IRA. You need about twenty minutes, a bank account, and a little bit of direction.

Most people put this off for years because retirement accounts sound complicated. They aren't. Once you understand how to start a Roth IRA, the actual process takes less time than setting up a new streaming subscription.

## Why a Roth IRA Is Worth Your Time

A Roth IRA is a retirement account funded with money you've already paid taxes on. In exchange, your investments grow completely tax-free, and you won't owe a dime when you withdraw that money in retirement.

Compare that to a regular brokerage account, where you pay taxes on dividends every year and again on any gains when you sell. A Roth IRA skips all of that. Set aside $300 a month starting at age 30, and by 65 you could have contributed $126,000. Depending on how your investments perform, the actual balance could be several times that, and every cent of the growth comes out tax-free.

## Step 1: Confirm You're Eligible

You need earned income—wages, salary, or self-employment income—to contribute to a Roth IRA. Investment income and Social Security don't count. Your modified adjusted gross income (MAGI) also determines whether you can contribute the full amount directly. If your income is too high, a "backdoor" Roth IRA allows you to contribute to a traditional IRA and then convert it to a Roth.

## Step 2: Choose Where to Open Your Account

You can open a Roth IRA at almost any brokerage, credit union, or robo-advisor. If you'd rather pick your own investments, a low-cost brokerage gives you access to thousands of funds with no account minimums and no annual fees. If you'd rather set it and forget it, a robo-advisor builds a diversified portfolio for you based on your age and risk tolerance, usually for a small annual fee.

## Step 3: Open the Account and Provide Your Information

Opening the account itself takes about ten minutes online. You'll need your Social Security number, a valid ID, and your bank account and routing numbers to fund it. You will also choose a beneficiary, the person who inherits the account if something happens to you. 

## Step 4: Fund the Account

You can fund it with a one-time transfer, or you can set up automatic monthly contributions from your checking account. Automatic contributions are the better move for most people. You never see the money, so you never miss it. Remember that contribution limits apply across all your IRAs combined, not per account.

## Step 5: Choose Your Investments

Opening a Roth IRA and leaving the cash sitting there uninvested is one of the most common mistakes. Cash sitting in a Roth IRA earns close to nothing. Invested money grows. For most people starting out, a low-cost target-date fund or a total market index fund is the simplest option.

## Key Takeaways

Opening a Roth IRA is simpler than most people expect. Confirm your eligibility, pick a provider, open the account, fund it, and choose investments that match your timeline. The account itself won't build your wealth. Consistent contributions and actual invested dollars will.

## Start Today

You don't need perfect timing or a large deposit to get started. Open your Roth IRA this week, set up an automatic transfer of whatever amount feels manageable, and choose a simple index fund or target-date fund to hold it.
