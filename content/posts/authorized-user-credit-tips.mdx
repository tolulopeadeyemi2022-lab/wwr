An authorized user is someone added to another person's credit card account who can use the card without being legally responsible for the debt. The account's history, including its age and payment record, often appears on the authorized user's credit report too, making it a powerful strategy for building a thin or damaged file.

## How Becoming an Authorized User Affects Your Score
Your credit score depends on several factors. Becoming an authorized user touches nearly all of them at once:
* **Payment history (35%):** An account with years of on-time payments sends a strong signal that gets inherited the moment you are added.
* **Credit utilization (30%):** Lower utilization on the primary account helps your overall credit picture.
* **Length of credit history (15%):** Getting added to an older account can add years to your average account age overnight.
* **Credit mix (10%):** Can round out your file slightly if your only other account is a starter card.

## Authorized User vs. Joint Account Holder
* **Authorized User:** Carries no legal responsibility for the balance, meaning you are not on the hook if a payment is missed.
* **Joint Account Holder:** Both names sit on the application, and both people are legally responsible for the balance and take the hit if a bill goes unpaid.

## What to Check Before You Become an Authorized User
* **Does the issuer report to all three bureaus?** Confirm that the issuer actually reports authorized user activity to Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion.
* **Is the account in good standing?** Avoid accounts with late payments or high utilization, which can hurt your score instead of helping.
* **How old is the account?** Older accounts add more value to your average credit age.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does becoming an authorized user hurt my credit score?
It can, but only if the primary account has late payments or high utilization. A well-managed account with a long history and low balance almost always helps.

### Do I need to use the card to get the credit benefit?
No. Many authorized users never receive a physical card or make a single purchase, as the reporting benefit comes from being listed on the account.

## Key Takeaways
Becoming an authorized user can add years of credit history and a stronger utilization ratio to your report almost immediately. Always vet the account's history and payment record before agreeing to anything.

## What To Do Next
Identify a trusted family member or spouse with a long-standing, well-managed credit card, ask if their issuer reports authorized user activity, and request to be added.
