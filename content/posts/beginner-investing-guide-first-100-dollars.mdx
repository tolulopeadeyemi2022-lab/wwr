You don't need $10,000 to start investing. You don't need to understand the stock market perfectly, either. You need $100, a brokerage account, and about fifteen minutes.

## Why Starting Small Still Matters

Compound interest is your money earning money, then earning money on those earnings. That's why starting early matters so much, even with a small amount.

Investing $100 today and adding $100 every month at a 7% average annual return grows to roughly $17,300 after ten years. Wait five years to start, and that same monthly contribution only reaches about $8,500 in the remaining time. The dollar amount barely changed. The timing changed everything.

## Step 1: Open a Brokerage Account

Before your $100 can do anything, it needs a home. A brokerage account is simply where you buy and hold investments, similar to how a bank account holds cash.

For most beginners, a taxable brokerage account or a Roth IRA are the two starting points worth considering.

- **Roth IRA.** Contributions grow tax-free, and withdrawals in retirement are tax-free too. This is usually the better starting point if you're investing for long-term goals like retirement.
- **Taxable brokerage account.** No contribution limits and no restrictions on withdrawals, but you'll owe taxes on gains. Better suited for goals outside retirement.

Major providers like Fidelity, Charles Schwab, and Vanguard all offer accounts with no minimum balance requirements and no account fees.

<Callout type="info" title="Which one should you pick?">
If you're investing for retirement and won't need the money for decades, start with a Roth IRA. If you're saving for a shorter-term goal like a house down payment in five to seven years, a taxable account offers more flexibility.
</Callout>

## Step 2: Choose an Index Fund, Not Individual Stocks

Diversification simply means spreading your investments across different assets, so one bad investment doesn't hurt your entire portfolio. An index fund does that automatically by holding hundreds or thousands of companies at once.

An S&P 500 index fund, for example, holds shares in roughly 500 of the largest U.S. companies. When you buy one share, you're effectively buying a small slice of all of them.

Picking individual stocks requires research most beginners haven't done yet, and it concentrates risk in a handful of companies instead of spreading it out. Index funds solve both problems at once, which is why they're usually the better starting point.

## Step 3: Put Your $100 to Work

With your account open and an index fund selected, buying is straightforward:

1. Transfer $100 from your bank account into your brokerage account.
2. Search for your chosen fund by its ticker symbol, such as VOO or VTI for total market index funds.
3. Enter the dollar amount you want to invest.
4. Confirm the purchase.

Most brokerages now allow fractional shares, meaning your $100 buys a partial share even if a full share costs more.

## Step 4: Automate Future Contributions

The first $100 matters less than what happens next. Set up an automatic monthly transfer, even if it's just $50 or $100, so investing becomes a habit rather than something you decide to do every month.

Automating removes the temptation to time the market or skip a month because something else feels more urgent. Consistency beats trying to invest at the "perfect" moment, because that moment rarely announces itself in advance.

## Common Beginner Investing Mistakes

**Waiting until you have "enough" money.** There's no minimum threshold that makes investing worthwhile. Starting with $100 beats waiting for $1,000 that may take years to save.

**Checking your account daily.** Short-term market swings are normal and meaningless for long-term investors. Checking constantly usually leads to emotional decisions, not better ones.

**Trying to pick winning stocks immediately.** Save individual stock picking, if you choose to do it at all, for after you've built a solid index fund foundation and can afford to take on more risk.

**Panic selling during a downturn.** Markets fluctuate. Selling during a dip locks in the loss. Staying invested gives your money time to recover and grow.

## How Much Should You Invest Each Month?

There's no universal number, but a reasonable starting target is 10 to 15% of your income once your emergency fund is in place and high-interest debt is under control. If that feels out of reach right now, start with whatever amount you can sustain consistently and increase it as your income grows.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I need to understand the stock market before I invest?**

No. You don't need to understand the stock market perfectly before you begin investing. An index fund handles diversification for you, and understanding deepens naturally over time.

**Is $100 really enough to start?**

Yes. What matters more than the starting amount is building the habit of contributing regularly. $100 today with consistent monthly additions outperforms waiting for a larger lump sum later.

**Should I pay off debt before investing?**

Pay off high-interest debt, generally anything above 7-8% interest, before investing aggressively. For lower-interest debt, it's often reasonable to do both at once.

## Key Takeaways

Beginner investing isn't complicated. Open an account, choose a broad index fund, invest what you can, and automate the process so consistency does the heavy lifting.

Open a brokerage account this week, even if you only transfer $100 to start. The habit you build matters more than the amount you begin with.
