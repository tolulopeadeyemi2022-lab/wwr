Paying down debt and building good credit are distinct tasks, but you don't have to choose between them. Mastering both means understanding what actually moves your score while managing what you owe.

## Why Paying Off Debt Doesn't Automatically Build Credit
Your credit score is calculated using specific factors, and debt payoff only directly influences a couple of them. While paying off a loan helps your utilization and payment history, closing the account right after can shorten your credit history and reduce your total available credit.

## Step 1: Keep Paying On Time
Payment history makes up the largest piece of your FICO score. Never let an extra payment on one account cause you to miss a minimum payment on another, as a single missed payment can stay on your report for up to seven years. Set up autopay for at least the minimum on every account to protect your score.

## Step 2: Watch Your Credit Utilization Closely
Credit utilization measures your balance against your credit limit. Lenders prefer this number below 30%, and under 10% is ideal. Knocking down revolving credit card balances produces faster score improvements than paying down installment loans.

## Step 3: Don't Close Old Accounts Once Paid Off
Closing a credit card immediately removes its available credit limit from your utilization calculation and shortens your average account age. Instead, keep the card open, put a small recurring charge on it, and set up autopay to maintain your credit history benefits.

## Step 4: Avoid Opening New Credit During Payoff
Every new application triggers a hard inquiry that can temporarily lower your score. Limit new applications unless you are intentionally consolidating high-interest debt into a lower-rate personal loan or 0% balance transfer card.

## Step 5: Consider Secured Cards or Credit Builder Loans
If your credit history is thin, secured credit cards or credit builder loans can help establish positive payment history while your existing debt is being paid down separately.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
* **Maxing out one card** to pay off another, which spikes your utilization ratio.
* **Missing a payment** to free up cash for aggressive debt payoff.
* **Closing every paid-off card** immediately, which can hurt your utilization and account age.
* **Ignoring your credit report** for errors that could drag down your score.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I build credit while still in debt?
Yes. Payment history and credit utilization both improve as you make on-time payments and reduce balances, even before your debt is fully paid off.

### How long does it take to see credit improvement?
Utilization changes can reflect within one billing cycle, while payment history improvements build gradually over several months of consistent, on-time payments.

## Key Takeaways
Balancing debt payoff and credit building requires keeping accounts current, focusing extra payments on lowering utilization, avoiding closing old cards, and limiting new credit applications.
