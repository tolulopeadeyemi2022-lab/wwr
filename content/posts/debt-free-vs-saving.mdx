Deciding whether to allocate your money toward paying off debt or building savings keeps many people stuck. The right approach is rarely all-or-nothing; instead, it depends on your interest rates, existing savings, and income stability.

## The Framework in Short
Build a small starter emergency fund before anything else, capture any employer matching funds, and then split your remaining money based on your interest rates.

## Step-by-Step Decision Guide

### 1. Build a Starter Cushion First
Set aside $500 to $1,000 in a basic savings account before aggressive debt payoff. This small buffer prevents unexpected expenses from forcing you right back onto your credit cards.

### 2. Compare Interest Rates
Compare your debt's interest rate against what your savings or investments earn. High-interest debt (such as credit cards at 20% to 29%) should take priority because it costs significantly more than any standard savings account yields.

### 3. Never Skip an Employer Match
Contribute enough to capture your full 401(k) employer match before tackling moderate-interest debt, as the return on a match is unmatched by standard payoff timelines.

### 4. Choose a Split Based on Your Debt Load
* **High Interest (Above 15%)**: Put the vast majority of extra funds toward debt while maintaining your starter cushion.
* **Moderate Interest (6% to 15%)**: Consider a split favoring debt, such as 70/30 or 60/40, based on your personal comfort.
* **Low Interest (Below 6%)**: Build savings and investments alongside minimum debt payments since the low interest cost offers high flexibility.

### 5. Factor In Income Stability
If your income is unpredictable or commission-based, lean toward a larger savings buffer before going aggressive on debt payoff.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
* **Treating all debt the same** regardless of widely varying interest rates.
* **Building a massive emergency fund** before paying off high-interest balances.
* **Skipping the employer match** in favor of aggressive debt elimination.
* **Waiting for a perfect split percentage** instead of taking action with a rough framework.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Should I pause all saving until debt is paid off?
Only for very high-interest debt (above 20%) after your starter cushion is in place; otherwise, some ongoing saving alongside payoff is wise.

### What interest rate justifies prioritizing debt?
Many people use 7% to 8% as a benchmark line, prioritizing debt payoff above that threshold.

## Key Takeaways
The debt versus saving dilemma is best resolved by building a small starter cushion, securing employer matches, and splitting remaining funds according to real interest rates and income stability.
