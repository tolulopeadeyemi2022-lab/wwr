Two people invest the exact same amount every month for the exact same number of years. One ends up with roughly double the money of the other. The only difference is when they started. This is the power of compound interest, and it is the single biggest reason why starting now matters more than the amount you start with.

## What Compound Interest Actually Means

Compound interest is your money earning money, and then earning money on those earnings too. Unlike simple interest—which only pays you based on your original principal—compound interest allows your balance to grow exponentially over time. Over thirty years at a 7% return, $1,000 grows to roughly $7,612 through compounding, compared to only $3,100 with simple interest.

## Why Time Matters More Than the Amount

The earlier you start, the more years your money has to compound. A ten-year head start can be the difference between hundreds of thousands of dollars in final wealth. For example, someone who starts investing at 25 and stops at 65 may end up with more than double the final balance of someone who starts at 35, even if both invested the same monthly amount. Ten years of lost compounding is a gap that is extremely difficult to close later in life without significantly increasing your contributions.

## The Rule of 72

If you want a quick way to estimate how long it takes your money to double, use the Rule of 72. Divide 72 by your expected annual return to find the approximate number of years until your investment doubles. At a 7% return, your money doubles in roughly 10 years.

## How to Put Compound Interest to Work

* **Start now:** Waiting for a bigger paycheck usually costs more than it saves because the earliest dollars have the most time to compound.
* **Reinvest earnings:** Ensure your dividends are set to reinvest automatically so your gains can continue to compound.
* **Contribute consistently:** Regular monthly contributions help smooth out market fluctuations and provide more "fuel" for compounding.
* **Choose low fees:** High fees compound against you just like growth compounds for you. Always opt for funds with low expense ratios.
* **Give it time:** Compounding rewards patience more than any other financial strategy.

## Common Mistakes That Slow Down Compounding

* **Pulling money out early:** Withdrawing investments interrupts the compounding process entirely.
* **Waiting for the "right time":** Ideal market conditions rarely exist, and the cost of waiting usually outweighs any perceived benefit.
* **Underestimating small amounts:** Even small, consistent contributions can compound into meaningful sums over decades.

## Your Next Step
